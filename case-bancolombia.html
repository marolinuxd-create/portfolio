01 · Problem

The bank with the most products had the most paper.

Bancolombia is the bank with the largest product portfolio in Colombia. To request a document, customers had to download a PDF, print it, fill it in by hand, sign it, scan it, and upload it back for every product. Operations rejected forms for typos, missing fields, or unreadable handwriting, and the loop started again.

The cost showed up everywhere: customer abandonment, back-office rework, support call volume, and zero data quality. The bank had a TO-BE proposal from an external vendor (Multiplica), but it needed grounding in real teams and real customers before any of it could ship.

The brief, in one line

Turn 60+ paper procedures into a digital experience that customers, the back office, and the bank's regulators all sign off on.

02 · My role

The bridge between vendor strategy and shippable design.

I led UX research and design. My job was to refine the TO-BE proposal, align 30 internal teams behind a Minimum Desired Experience, design the new flow against the bank's design system, and document every accessibility decision so the dev team could implement it without guessing.

03 · Process

Two phases: align the org, then design the experience.

P.1

Phase 1 — Refine the TO-BE proposal

Sessions with Product Owners, Solution Architects, and developers to score every proposed feature on a 4-quadrant matrix: implementation effort (1–5) vs. experience happiness (1–5).

  • "Do now" — high happiness, low effort
  • "Plan for it" — high happiness, high effort
  • "Structure & resource" — low happiness, high effort
  • "Manageable now" — low happiness, low effort

Then 30 co-creation sessions with the operational teams who own each step — to identify needs, refine requirements, and stress-test the vendor proposal.

P.2

Phase 2 — Design, test, document for accessibility

Backlog for design and dev. Prototypes built against the bank's design system. Continuous testing using usability tests, first-click tests, surveys, interviews, cardsorting, and an eye-tracker.

At the end, full accessibility documentation written with the A11y Focus Order Figma plugin, screen-by-screen, for keyboard and screen-reader users handed off to dev as a spec, not a suggestion. We also redesigned a key form using colorless styling tactics (line patterns instead of color signals) to meet WCAG criteria for color independence.

04 · Key decisions

The four trade-offs that earned the team's trust.

Decision 01

E-signature first, scan as fallback.

Adobe Experience Manager forms with embedded electronic signature became the default path. Document upload stayed only as a regulated fallback for specific products.

Decision 02

One personal-document component, used everywhere.

Instead of redesigning the upload step per procedure, I designed a single reusable component for personal document upload — adopted by every product team that ships forms.

Decision 03

Accessibility annotated in design, not after.

Focus order, ARIA roles, and screen-reader copy were specified inside the Figma file  same source of truth as visual design. Dev couldn't ship without them.

Decision 04

Co-create the prioritization, don't dictate it.

The 4-quadrant matrix was built with 30 teams, not handed to them. Adoption was instant because each team had voted on what shipped.

05 · Before & after

The shift customers and the back office felt.

Before

Download, print, sign, scan, upload, repeat.

Up to 60 paper-based procedures per product family. High rejection rate from operations. Inaccessible to keyboard and screen-reader users.

After

Open form, sign, submit. Done.

Digital form components with e-signature. Single document-upload pattern reused across the bank. WCAG-aligned focus order, screen-reader copy, and color-independent feedback shipped to dev.

06 · Impact

What changed for users, the team, and the bank.

30 co-creation teams aligned
0 paper steps in new flow
1 reusable upload component
WCAG A11y docs shipped per screen
What I'd do differently

Run a 1-week parallel form study before phase 2. We discovered late that some users wanted a saved-progress affordance for long forms. Catching that earlier would have saved a redesign cycle on three flows.
