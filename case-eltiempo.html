01 · Problem

A century of trust, suddenly without a business model.

El Tiempo had been Colombia's #1 newspaper since 1911. The pandemic flipped two numbers at the same time: page views on eltiempo.com soared, while print circulation the actual revenue engine collapsed.

The team had loyal readers, but no easy way to ask them to pay. Earlier soft attempts at metered access had felt punitive and confused both readers and the sales team. We needed a real subscription product, not a wall.

The brief, in one line

Convert 30,000 readers into paying digital subscribers in year 1 — without alienating the audience that made El Tiempo what it is.

02 · My role

Lead the research, then design the product around what we learned.

I owned end-to-end research for the subscription experience and led design on the plans + paywall. I worked alongside another UX researcher, a UI designer, and three developers and partnered closely with editorial, commercial, and the alliances team that owned partner benefits.

My specific deliverables: research plan, archetype definition, plan architecture, paywall flow, and the responsive landing pages that brought it all together.

03 · Process

Research wide, ideate hard, prioritize honestly.

Three phases over 12 months. The work that mattered happened in phase 2 turning 170 raw ideas into a roadmap the business could actually ship.

P.1

Discovery — quant + qual in parallel

We pulled audience behavior from Google Analytics, mapped every business asset and partner alliance, and interviewed key stakeholders to understand what was already on the table.

  • Online survey distributed across reader communities (2 weeks)
  • Remote moderated interviews + cardsorting with 30 users across 5 archetypes
  • Stakeholder interviews — editorial, commercial, alliances, tech
P.2

Ideation 170 ideas → 70 solutions → 1 roadmap

We ran ideation sessions with stakeholders and translated insights into a backlog of 170 ideas, grouped into 70 solutions across 9 benefit categories: traffic, offers, gifts, events, sales, training, and others. Each was scored on impact vs. effort with the tech team. The result: a phased roadmap and named plans grounded in what users would actually pay for.

P.3

Design + test iterate on real flows

I sketched, then moved to low-fi wireframes in Adobe XD. After 5 rounds of usability tests we tightened copy and hierarchy, then built hi-fi responsive prototypes for the paywall, plan comparison, and checkout. Microcopy was rewritten with editorial every line had to sound like El Tiempo, not a SaaS startup.

04 · Key decisions

The four trade-offs I'd defend in any review.

Decision 01

Lead with benefits, not features.

Plan cards were re-architected around the partner benefits users said they'd pay for, not the article counts the team initially favored. Article limits stayed in the fine print.

Decision 02

Three plans, not five.

Research showed cognitive overload at 5 tiers. We collapsed to Basic / Plus / Total, each anchored to a clear archetype, with a fourth "print + digital" option discoverable but not loud.

Decision 03

Paywall after value, never before.

The wall triggers after the reader has finished an article not before. Conversion went up; complaint volume to support went down.

Decision 04

Plan names came from users.

Stakeholders proposed clever editorial names. Users found them confusing. We tested 8 names with 5 archetypes and shipped the three users could repeat back without prompting.

05 · Before & after

The shift readers felt.

Before

"Wait, I have to pay now?"

Metered wall blocked articles mid-scroll. Plans page listed article counts, three CTAs above the fold, no clear differentiation. Cancel rate after first month: high.

After

"Oh, I get all of this?"

Wall appears after value delivery. Plans organized by archetype with named partner benefits up top. One primary CTA per card. Year-1 retention beat industry benchmarks.

06 · Impact

The numbers we promised  then doubled, tripled.

100K digital subscribers, year 1
3.3× over the original 30K target
30 interviews · 5 archetypes
9 benefit categories shipped
What I'd do differently

Validate pricing earlier. We anchored plan prices on commercial assumptions and only stress-tested them in usability tests. A dedicated pricing study in week 4 would've saved two redesigns later.
